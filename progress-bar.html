Project Overview

Context

Welcome to Giftd, a circular economy Berlin based start-up and social network app available on iOS and Android. Gift your pre-loved clothes to loved ones and neighbors, be part of a close-knit community and receive high-value clothes for free. Combat the growing carbon footprint caused by clothing waste with Giftd.

Timeline

January - Shipped in March 2023

Team

1 UX/UI Designer - Me, 1 Founder, 1 Co Founder and CTO

2 Marketing Team

Problem

As a part of Giftd's roadmap, I was tasked with designing a feature that would not only enhance user engagement but also make them invested and interested in Giftd's mission.

The problem we were trying to solve was how to connect users to Giftd mission while celebrating the top 3-4% of engaged users with the greatest impact?

Impact

I got to work and collaborated closely with the founder, developer, and marketing team to design a reward experience that would benefit both primary users (a.k.a GIFTD ambassadors) and secondary users.

The solution is simple yet effective - I designed an experience that shows users how through gifting clothes, they and their community are progressing towards CO2e neutrality.

Discover the Issue

How can we celebrate the top 3-4% of engaged users with the greatest impact?

As a first step I asked the founder clarifying questions: What are we trying to solve with this feature? What is the user goal? What value are we providing to users and to the product by building this feature?

From the conversation I’ve identified the desired outcome with the feature, which is to enhance user engagement, for both primary and secondary users, while celebrating the top 3-4% engaged users with the greatest impact.

I was really excited to have the opportunity to brainstorm the Hook Model

As a designer, I am fascinated by the intersection of psychology and design, and the potential it holds for creating truly engaging experiences. My curiosity led me to dive deep into the psychology of motivation and engagement which led me to discover the Hook Model, a framework for creating habit-forming products that consists of four steps.

I was thrilled to brainstorm ideas using the Hook Model framework with the founder and developer.

As we explored the four steps, we focused on understanding what rewards primary users desire and how we can design to meet those needs. We also considered how the rewards are linked to the impact primary users want to see on GIFTD.

From the brainstorm session we learned:

01. Gamification elements can be used to enhance engagement

02. For gamification to work we need to uncover the real motivation / goal of the user

03. We must experiment with creating a loop of triggered actions and unpredictable rewards for users

GIFTD Super Users

“I want to see my impact on contributing to the growing community of gifting”

Through our use of the Hook Model and previous user research, we developed 3 assumptions about what is important to users in terms of seeing progress and the impact they want to have as ambassadors.

Although these assumptions were based on data from previous research, we decided to validate them with our primary users before beginning the design process.

What we learned:

90%

Our users are most incentivized by their environmental impact

Our users want to see the wider community impact they have by inviting others

10%

Our users are less interested in seeing how friends of friends are making an impact

Joining Forces

How can we emotionally connect users to their CO2e savings through the user interface?

Ideation is one of my favorite parts as I really enjoy brainstorming with my colleagues towards the same goal, it’s also very inspiring to hear other people’s thoughts and building on each others ideas.

One of the questions that drove my designs during ideation was: how through the user interface to emotionally connect users to their CO2e impact?

During the ideation session we had an “aha” moment and thought to use metaphors and numbers to show the progress users have as they invite friends and gift items.

Design explorations

Introducing Community Impact

How can design and psychology be used in a progress bar?

In addition to visually representing top users with a badge, we also implemented a progress bar for users to track their own progress and that of their community. The progress bar is linked to users' motivation to show and contribute towards reducing CO2e emissions, while also allowing them to see how their friends are contributing to the cause.

Short term rewards and Goal gradient effect

By making use of the Goal Gradient Effect, which suggests that breaking down tasks into smaller parts with incremental rewards can be more motivating than not having any visual cues or rewards, I split the progress bar into milestones and celebrate users when each milestone has been passed.

Community Impact

The Community Impact Screen is where users can track their own progress and that of their community towards CO2e reduction, while also offering invitations that grant one month of free reservations.

Metaphors vs. numbers or “People remember stories better than facts alone”

We aimed to make the experience of observing and monitoring your impact enjoyable, so we came up with the idea to use an empty human icon that gradually fills up as you make progress

With a progress bar, are we encouraging bad behavior? And if yes, how can we prevent it?

While designing the progress bar, I also considered its potential downsides and wondered how I could prevent negative emotions and unethical behavior.

01. Transparent: communicate what the bar represents and what it is measuring and how

02. Fake progress bar: make sure that you’re tracking in real time

03. Provide clear feedback to users when a process is complete or if there is an error

04. Accessible: use high contrast colors to make it easier for users to see

Impact

Through close collaboration with the founder, developer, and marketing team, I designed an experience that shows users how through gifting clothes, they and their community are progressing towards CO2e neutrality.
