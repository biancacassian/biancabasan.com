Project Overview

Context

Giftd is an app, available on iOS and android, through which you can gift your pre-loved clothes to friends, family and neighbors. More than 3600 downloads and over 2000 gifts have been made since it started in November 2021.

Take a look - www.giftd.app

Timeline

Sept 2022 - ongoing

Team

1 UX/UI Designer - Me, 1 Founder, 1 Co Founder and CTO

My involvement with Giftd started in July and it is ongoing, since then I have been taking on a couple of challenges.

As the sole UX/UI designer I am responsible for ​​improving existing flows, working on new features, initial discovery and iterate design solutions at all levels of fidelity. In this case study I will talk about how I approached challenges such as:

01. Optimizing and iterating on the onboarding flow

02. Designing the first filtering option

03. Designing the delivery feature

Onboarding

It all started with... Finding an opportunity for improving the onboarding experience

Giftd is a product that listens to its users feedback, and so for my first meeting with the founder I decided to bring my experience, as a user, as I was going through the onboarding flow.

Based on the data collected on the current onboarding flow that the founder shared with me 90% of users succeed in the process with just 10% abandoning, and users generally find it to be easy and fast. What the current onboarding is doing is the set up moment, creating an account, granting permission, asking for personal information, these actions are necessary however they don’t provide immediate benefits.

What I noticed that was missing from the current onboarding was communicating the value that I get as a user from using Giftd. Since the product is constantly evolving, together with the founder we examined the present flow to seek for opportunities and eliminate stages that are not essential.

Here is what we found:

01. Opportunity: How can we communicate the value of the product?

02. Opportunity / Problem: Users don’t know exactly what they are supposed to upload

03. Problem: As a new user joining without invite vs. user joining via invite, the flow looks different and is confusing

Goals and Objectives

How might we allow users to easily connect with their friends and family

How might we communicate to users the value they get out of Giftd

How might we encourage users to post their first item during onboarding

Research

Doing desk research and checking in with users

With the objections and goals clarified, I started by conducting a competitive analysis that helped me to look into strategic insights, flows and design solutions of competitor’s onboarding. The main objective of the competitive analysis was to look into how users connect with friends and family during onboarding.

As I was doing the research, a survey was sent out to the users to identify pain points, motivations and overall experience. I then collected and identified the most common pain points, requests and areas of improvement. In regards to the onboarding flow, users didn’t express any pain points and the typical requirement is for it to be quick with a complete setup.

Competitive analysis chart

People born during a specific time period have similar values, habits, and ways of thinking

When considering how to communicate the value of Giftd to users, I conducted desk research by reading articles and marketing blogs. I've learned from marketing strategy that if we want to understand how to communicate the value of Giftd to users, we must first identify the users' generations. Because the values, habits, and thinking styles of people born during a particular generation are similar.

Having the questions answered will help us make better decisions, ensuring that we are aligned on what are the measurable key performance indicators and what are the business and user goals in regards to the new solution. In addition we have also reviewed the current flow.

Design explorations

Filters

Implementing the first filters and running into problems

One of the main pain points I found when gathering the survey's data was that there was no filtering option, which made it difficult for users to find items that were available, local, or published by friends.

Together with the founder and the developer, we decided to start by allowing users to filter by friends, reserved, public and gifted items and expand on the next MVP.

I was excited to design the filtering options and have users use it, but after only one week users began to contact us with concerns: "I think there is a bug since I can't see anything when I am filtering."

When designing the mockups, I failed to take user journeys and flows into account. I didn't think about edge cases such as: what would the user see if they tried to filter just by friends but didn't have any? With the current solution users will be encountered by an empty screen with no visual guidance, which causes confusion.

Design explorations

As a potential solution I would have added text to the empty screen directing the user back to the filtering options. The text would be written in a friendly tone, indicating why the screen is blank and what to do next.

Learnings

What this experience taught me is that when the design gets into the real world there will always be something to learn from that we won't know about until users start using it.

Delivery Feature

Expand beyond location

Up until now, Giftd users would schedule meetings via DMs with other users in order to receive the items. The goal of having a shipping option besides users meeting offline with each other is to provide more options for transacting items and expand beyond our current location (Berlin).

Designing the first delivery feature was an exciting and challenging experience that I approached first by returning to what I had learned from improving the onboarding flow and designing the filtering option.

Here are some of the factors that I considered:

01. Focus on user’s goal by making sure we have a detailed understanding of exactly what users need at each step of the task.

02. Put users first through user stories.

03. Talk with the developer to understand the technical limitations.

Discovery

When paying for something online, the user will have two thoughts: is this safe and is this clear?

In order to understand how a successful shipping and online payment experience can be designed I have looked at competitors and I have also read customers reviews which have been beneficial in order to understand what their pain points are.

I have also conducted initial desk research through articles to learn about best practices for mobile payment experiences, as well as looking into UI considerations for a successful payment and delivery feature.

A glimpse at the user stories

As a result of the initial research, I was able to build user stories and consider the user flow together with the founder.

We brainstormed user stories from the perspectives of a gifter (the person giving away) and a receiver ( the user interested in the item).

Some questions that came to mind:

01.What is the user trying to accomplish at each step?

02. How can we build trust when making a payment?

03. How can we ensure that users understand that they are only paying for shipping and not the item?

Working With Others

Iteration & Collaboration

With the user stories ready I couldn't move forward without mapping out the flow of both the gifter and the receiver. Seeing the complete flow, from both users, made it easier to identify tasks needed at each step of the process.

The low-fidelity mock-ups were presented to the developer for feedback, and we later conducted user testing with two end users. The goal of user testing was to ensure that everything is clear and that there are no barriers preventing users from accomplishing their goals of uploading an item, selecting an item for delivery, and making a payment.

A sneak peek at the mock-up flow

We moved on to the development phase after the testing was completed successfully.

In terms of the UI, I was guided by the psychological principle of Social Norms in which people expect most online interactions to follow the same social rules as person-to-person interactions.

Impact

The feature is currently in progress and is going to be live in the beginning of February.
